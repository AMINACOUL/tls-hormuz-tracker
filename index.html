✅ Finding 1 — No Subsidy at Current Prices
Decree ceilings ($1.50 petrol, $1.65 diesel) are above current market prices at Brent $95. The government pays $0 in subsidies. Distributors earn margin of $0.31/L above landed cost. This is a consumer price cap, not a fiscal transfer.
💡 Finding 2 — Subsidy Breakevens Are Very High
Diesel ceiling triggered at Brent $158/bbl. Petrol at $184/bbl. Both far above crisis peak ($119). Only at extreme scenarios does a tiny subsidy emerge (~$1.5M/yr at $165). The decree ceilings were set very conservatively.
⚠ Finding 3 — Strategic Reserve IS a Fiscal Cost ($168.8M)
The 80M L diesel strategic reserve (1 Apr 2026) at $2.11/L is purchased at elevated crisis prices — this is a supply security expenditure, not a subsidy. Opportunity cost vs. pre-crisis price: ~$70M if Brent returns to $75. ESI ($563M) more than covers it.
🏛️ Finding 4 — Consistent with ADB/IMF Policy
ADB Brief #384 and IMF WEO both recommend avoiding "broad-based price controls" in favor of targeted support. TL's approach — ceiling caps without direct fiscal transfers — is aligned with this advice. ESI ($563M/yr) and PF ($18.77bn) provide full fiscal buffer for all plausible scenarios.

Why Food Prices Matter for Timor-Leste

Food is 64% of the TL CPI basket (INETL Series 3) — one of the highest shares globally. Rice alone accounts for 17% of CPI with imports covering approximately 60–90% of domestic cereal consumption, sourced primarily from India, Vietnam, and China, with annual volumes typically 135,000–160,000 tonnes (OEC World / FAO GIEWS). An IMF working paper (2026) confirms that global food and rice prices are the single most important drivers of TL inflation, with near-immediate pass-through given dollarisation. The Hormuz crisis creates a dual food risk: (1) direct shipping disruption to TL food imports from India, Vietnam & China; (2) fertilizer supply shock — 30% of globally traded fertilizers transit Hormuz — threatening 2026–27 rice yields in key supplier countries, particularly India. FAO Chief Economist (27 Mar): fertilizer prices surging — FOB Egypt urea rose from $400–490 to ~$700/t (+68%). Middle East granular urea +19% in first week of March; Egyptian urea +28%. FAO projects fertilizer prices 15–20% higher in H1 2026 if crisis persists. 🛢️ Pourquoi MOPS et pas seulement le Brent ?
Le Timor-Leste n'achète pas du pétrole brut — il importe du fuel raffiné (essence, diesel) depuis Singapour. Le prix de référence pertinent est le MOPS (Mean of Platts Singapore) — prix des produits raffinés dans le hub régional. Le MOPS peut diverger du Brent : pendant la crise de Hormuz, le diesel MOPS a surpassé $200/bbl tandis que le Brent restait sous $120. Ce tracker utilise une approximation linéaire : MOPS Gasoline 92 ≈ $0.077 + $0.0102 × Brent. Un abonnement Platts (~$5,000/an) donnerait des données MOPS temps réel. Implication TL : le prix réel payé par PITSA/ETO peut être plus élevé que l'estimation Brent.
