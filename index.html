Situation Report

Brent fell to ~$98.83/bbl and WTI to ~$90.67 on March 24 — retreating from the crisis peak but remaining ~45% above the pre-crisis baseline. Timor-Leste’s ANPM-regulated ceiling prices rose on March 9: Petrol RON91 $1.10→$1.23 (+11.8%); Diesel $1.05→$1.22 (+16.2%). Observed market prices at some Dili stations have exceeded these ceilings (diesel reported at $1.40/L on Mar 11 — see Sources & Method tab for data notes). PM Xanana Gusmão committed to year-end supply security (Mar 19) and signalled prices “not expected to increase.” Pertamina next cargo arrives Mar 25–27. No subsidy introduced; Petroleum Fund at $18.77bn. The Diplomat (Mar 2026) identifies Timor-Leste explicitly among the region’s most exposed economies alongside Cambodia, Laos, and Myanmar. ASEAN economic ministers have warned that disruptions are “leading to higher freight, insurance, and logistics costs and contributing to inflationary pressures on energy, food, and other essential goods” across Southeast Asia.

Key Watchpoints

Questions that will shape the next update

1 — Subsidy / Intervention Decision
Will the government introduce a formal price cap or subsidy?
PM’s Mar 19 statement is a political signal, not a formal directive. Watch for an ANPM announcement. Any subsidy would have fiscal sustainability implications given the Petroleum Fund’s finite horizon.
2 — New Cargo Pricing (Mar 25–27)
Will the incoming Pertamina cargo trigger a second official ceiling adjustment?
New stock will be priced at current (higher) Singapore spot rates. Watch for an ANPM announcement in early April.
3 — Brent Price Trajectory
Will the retreat below $100/bbl hold?
Current: $98.83. The Trump postponement of further Iran strikes is the key variable. Sustained easing could reduce pressure for a second TL adjustment. IEA Executive Director Birol: “the greatest global energy security challenge in history.”
5 — Regional Supply Chain Spillovers
Will regional policy responses affect TL’s supply chain?
The Diplomat identifies TL as among the most exposed in SEA alongside Cambodia, Laos & Myanmar. Key risks: China banned diesel/gasoline/aviation fuel exports (pushing SEA buyers to scramble); Thailand banned oil exports except to Cambodia/Laos; Indonesia subsidies under severe pressure. Australia/Singapore supply chain deal (Mar 24) is positive for TL’s Singapore-sourced imports.
4 — ANPM Ceiling Enforcement
Will ANPM act on above-ceiling market prices?
Market diesel was reported at $1.40 vs $1.22 ceiling at some stations (Mar 11). ANPM has regulatory powers. Enforcement action or ceiling revision would both be significant developments.

Why Food Prices Matter for Timor-Leste

Food is 64% of the TL CPI basket (INETL Series 3) — one of the highest shares globally. Rice alone accounts for 17% of CPI with imports covering approximately 60–90% of domestic cereal consumption, sourced primarily from India, Vietnam, and China, with annual volumes typically 135,000–160,000 tonnes (OEC World / FAO GIEWS). An IMF working paper (2026) confirms that global food and rice prices are the single most important drivers of TL inflation, with near-immediate pass-through given dollarisation. The Hormuz crisis creates a dual food risk: (1) direct shipping disruption to TL food imports from India, Vietnam & China; (2) fertilizer supply shock — 30% of globally traded fertilizers transit Hormuz — threatening 2026–27 rice yields in key supplier countries, particularly India. Fertilizer prices estimated up 15–20% (FAO, Mar 2026).

Key Assumptions & Limitations

Judgment
TL market price observations are from isolated press reports (TATOLI, Lusa), not a systematic survey. The $1.40 diesel figure is from two named Dili stations on one date (Mar 11). Actual prices vary significantly across stations and districts. Some stations (e.g. David Freedman’s local station) report petrol at $1.20 — below the $1.23 ceiling. These figures are indicative only.
Judgment
Hormuz transit data from HormuzTracker / AISstream.io counts AIS-tracked vessels. Military or untracked vessels may be understated. The ‘normal’ baseline of 138 ships/day is the Feb 2026 average before the crisis.
Judgment
Historical global market data (Brent, WTI, Gold, Singapore spot) uses indicative values for trend illustration. Verify against primary sources (ICE, NYMEX, LBMA) before citing in official publications.
Judgment
Asia comparison figures for non-TL countries are sourced from the Vietnam Hormuz Tracker and EAP Fuel Policy database; not independently verified for this dashboard.
Judgment
Gold data context: Gold peaked at ~$5,594/oz in January 2026 due to factors predating the crisis. It has fallen ~23% since that peak to ~$4,320 by Mar 24. This counter-trend move reflects partial risk-off reversal and should not be interpreted as crisis-driven appreciation.
